Max Planck Institute for the Structure and Dynamics of Matter (MPSD)

Hamburg, Germany. Spring 2016

I spent the spring semester of 2016 as part of the Miller Group at the Max Planck Institute for the Structure and Dynamics of Matter (MPSD). Working as a support engineering intern at one of the world's foremost research institutions, I designed, built and installed optical and mechanical systems that contribute to ultrafast infrared laser experiments. My main project was the design, construction, and installation of a frequency-resolved optical gating (FROG) device for near-infrared wavelength lasers.

Apart from learning mechanical, electrical, and optical design principles, working at MPSD allowed me to get a feel for how work at a research institution differs from industry work. My co-workers, nearly all holding doctorates or pursuing them, were willing to share their extensive knowledge of physics and engineering with me, and I learned a lot. For example, did you know that the German word for threaded screws is "Zylinderschrauben"?

IBM

Poughkeepsie, NY. Summer 2015

After my first year of university I spent the summer working in the Input/Output Drawer Hardware Design department of Z Systems at IBM Poughkeepsie. During my tenure, I was responsible for the implementation of a database search tool that is still in use in the department. As the only software worker in a team of hardware engineers, I learned the valuable skill of initiative, since there was no one to ask for help.

Working at the world's leading mainframe designer and completely controlling my own project was exhilarating. I set up and ran meetings with other employees, regularly briefed my superiors, and taught myself 3 programming languages. At IBM, I learned the ins and outs of navigating a massive company, and making my own unique contribution.

The Robot Competition

University of British Columbia. Summer 2016

Engineering Physics, my degree program, is famous at UBC for its robot competition, where students build robots for a round-robin competition. My team of four built a successful robot, winning two matches before its unlucky defeat. Check out our team website.

I was responsible for mechanical design and construction, including conceptualizing the design and creating a comprehensive SolidWorks model. I also manufactured the robot with a laser cutter, waterjet machine, mill and lathe, and various hand tools. I was also involved in electrical and software design and implementation. The robot competition was a fun way to gain practical engineering experience, often learning design principles the hard way!

UBC Sailbot

University of British Columbia. Fall 2014-Summer 2016

I was a member of the UBC Sailbot team, which designed and built a robotic sailboat for the task of autonomously crossing the Atlantic Ocean. Named Ada, the boat set sail from Newfoundland in August of 2016, but unfortunately failed to reach its goal of Ireland.

I worked on the mechanical design and construction team. My design work mostly concerned installation and control methods for Ada's sails. I also was heavily involved in construction, including a lot of woodwork! I will never forget those endless hours spent sanding her to a smooth surface before applying the outer carbon-fibre layer.


Mechanical Design

Particularly developed during my time at MPSD and the ENPH253 robot competition, mechanical design is my strongest engineering discipline. I have extensive experience in computer-aided design (CAD) programs. In particular, I have worked a great deal with SolidWorks and Solid Edge, and other programs to a lesser degree. While specific controls change, all CAD programs share similar characteristics.

I also enjoy learning about mechanical engineering by working with my hands. During the ENPH253 robot course, I learned the basics of machine tools including metal lathes and mills, and developed skills with waterjet and laser cutters. During my time with UBC Sailbot, I helped construct our boat, including woodwork and carbon-fibre installation, among other things. I also enjoy tinkering on my own, and am becoming quite handy!

On top of practical skills, I have also taken a variety of mechanical engineering courses at UBC, including materials analysis and mechanical design classes. Applying the skills I learn in the classroom to practical work helps me knit all my mechanical knowledge together.

Programming and Computer Engineering

Since high school, I have been a proficient programmer. My first technical job, at IBM, involved database interfacing and data processing, in a combination of SQL, Groovy, and other programming languages. I am also working as a teaching assistant in a programming course in Java.

I believe that any modern engineer worth their salt should know their way around a computer, and be able to implement at least some simple coding. Personal programming projects are things that I sometimes work on for fun, in my spare time. Check out my Github account for a quick example. For another example, take a look around! What I've learned from my experience in coding is that, no matter how many programming languages you know, there will always be another you need to learn. That's why I try to notice similarities between languages (and there are many!) to facilitate gaining a quick, working knowledge of a new language. In this way, I was able to become somewhat proficient in SQL in one day while working at IBM, and get started on my project.

Electrical Skills

A good knowledge of circuitry had served me well both at university and in my work. One of my prouder moments working at MPSD was when I came up with the idea for a simple circuit that was later able to synchronize our laser firing with other equipment. When I later implemented my idea and installed it, the experiment's efficiency was more than doubled!

Other than working at MPSD, my current work at the Jones lab mostly involves building support circuitry. I have thus far built circuits intended to control piezo-motor motions, accouting for micro-adjustments in the cavity length of the lab's laser. It's fun to go to the lab and do a bit of circuit design as a study break during the semester, working different parts of my brain and keeping up my electrical skills. I also participated in circuitry for my team's robot during the ENPH253 competition, though my main contribution to the robot was mechanical design.

So far during my university education, I have taken several circuit analysis courses, including both practical design and circuit element analysis classes. It is these courses which gave me the practical knowledge that I use every day in the Jones lab, and helped me with my successes at MPSD.
